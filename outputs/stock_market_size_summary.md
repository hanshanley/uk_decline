# UK vs US stock-market size — trend summary

Coverage: **1975–2025** across 6 regions. Sources: World Bank WDI, World Bank WDI + World Bank (FP.CPI.TOTL, US CPI) (no API key). The comparison is anchored on the UK vs the US; World / EU / Japan / China are shown for context.
Real (inflation-adjusted) figures are in **constant 2024 US$**, the nominal series deflated by US CPI (FP.CPI.TOTL).


## Stock-market capitalisation (nominal) (current US$)

Total market value of listed domestic companies, in current (nominal) US dollars. The headline measure of absolute size, but not inflation-adjusted.

| Region | First | Latest |
|---|---|---|
| United Kingdom | $86B (1975) | $3.10T (2022) |
| United States | $704B (1975) | $68.94T (2025) |
| World | $1.15T (1975) | $141.30T (2025) |
| European Union | $166B (1975) | $5.83T (2018) |
| Japan | $142B (1975) | $7.61T (2025) |
| China | $513B (2003) | $15.51T (2025) |

**UK / US:** 8% in 2022 (peak 27% in 1990).

_Source: World Bank WDI — indicator CM.MKT.LCAP.CD._

> Caveat: Compiled by the World Bank from S&P Global / World Federation of Exchanges data. Denominated in current (nominal) US$, so it blends local-currency valuation, USD exchange-rate moves, and inflation. The WDI series has tail-year gaps -- the UK is missing after 2022 -- so recent years may be blank; values are not spliced. See the real series for the inflation-adjusted view.

## Stock-market cap-to-GDP ratio (% of GDP)

Market capitalisation of listed domestic companies as a share of GDP -- the size of the market relative to the wider economy. A same-year ratio, so it is inflation-neutral by construction.

| Region | First | Latest |
|---|---|---|
| United Kingdom | 35% (1975) | 97% (2022) |
| United States | 42% (1975) | 224% (2025) |
| World | 27% (1975) | 149% (2025) |
| European Union | 11% (1975) | 50% (2018) |
| Japan | 26% (1975) | 172% (2025) |
| China | 30% (2003) | 80% (2025) |

**UK / US:** 63% in 2022 (peak 175% in 1987).

_Source: World Bank WDI — indicator CM.MKT.LCAP.GD.ZS._

> Caveat: The more comparable 'size relative to the economy' measure, but sensitive to the denominator: countries with many large foreign-listed firms (e.g. the UK) can look large relative to domestic GDP.
