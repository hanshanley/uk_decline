# UK vs US stock-market size — trend summary

Coverage: **1975–2025** across 6 regions. Source: World Bank WDI (no API key). The comparison is anchored on the UK vs the US; World / EU / Japan / China are shown for context.


## Stock-market capitalisation (current US$)

Total market value of listed domestic companies, in current US dollars. The headline measure of the absolute size of a national stock market.

| Region | First | Latest |
|---|---|---|
| United Kingdom | $86B (1975) | $3.10T (2022) |
| United States | $704B (1975) | $68.94T (2025) |
| World | $1.15T (1975) | $141.30T (2025) |
| European Union | $166B (1975) | $5.83T (2018) |
| Japan | $142B (1975) | $7.61T (2025) |
| China | $513B (2003) | $15.51T (2025) |

**UK / US:** 8% in 2022 (peak 27% in 1990).

> Caveat: Compiled by the World Bank from S&P Global / World Federation of Exchanges data. Denominated in current US$, so it blends local-currency valuation and USD exchange-rate moves. The WDI series has tail-year gaps -- the UK is missing after 2022 -- so recent years may be blank; values are not spliced.

## Stock-market cap-to-GDP ratio (% of GDP)

Market capitalisation of listed domestic companies as a share of GDP -- the size of the market relative to the wider economy.

| Region | First | Latest |
|---|---|---|
| United Kingdom | 35% (1975) | 97% (2022) |
| United States | 42% (1975) | 224% (2025) |
| World | 27% (1975) | 149% (2025) |
| European Union | 11% (1975) | 50% (2018) |
| Japan | 26% (1975) | 172% (2025) |
| China | 30% (2003) | 80% (2025) |

**UK / US:** 63% in 2022 (peak 175% in 1987).

> Caveat: The more comparable 'size relative to the economy' measure, but sensitive to the denominator: countries with many large foreign-listed firms (e.g. the UK) can look large relative to domestic GDP.

**UK / US:** 35% in 2022 (peak 113% in 1979).

> Caveat: Counts domestic listings only; delistings, M&A, and take-private activity reduce the count even as market value rises. Compare trends, not raw counts, across exchanges with different listing rules.
